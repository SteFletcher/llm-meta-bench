llm-meta-bench · intent comparison · solar-bench

Same prompt, two solar systems.

Two models were handed the identical brief — a single sprawling spec for a “AAA-quality, browser-based, real-time Solar System simulator” that should feel like a game, not a website. Each owned the whole build. To compare intent versus execution, I started both apps, used each one's own on-screen navigation to fly to Earth, and captured the frame.

Brief: solar-bench/PROMPT.md Stack asked: Three.js · TypeScript · Vite Target: cinematic · scientifically accurate Task: navigate → Earth → screenshot

Captured with Playwright driving headless Chromium (WebGL2 via SwiftShader) at 1600×900: load the app, wait for boot, click the app's own Earth navigation control, let the cinematic fly-to settle, screenshot. Same procedure for both — the only variable is the code each model wrote.

Claude Fable 5

sol-simulator · “SOL”
Closest to intent

Got right

  • A believable Earth. Procedural surface with recognizable continents, blue specular oceans, layered drifting clouds and a thin atmospheric limb — rendered in-shader, not a flat sphere.
  • Cinematic framing. The fly-to arrived on the day side with Earth centered and fully lit — exactly the “leave it on a second monitor” feel the brief asked for.
  • Science is all there. Radius 6,371 km, gravity 9.81 m/s², escape velocity 11.19 km/s, orbital speed 29.78 km/s, axial tilt 23.44°, rotation 23.9 h, atmosphere N₂/O₂/Ar — correct and complete.
  • Deep HUD. Ten time scales with reverse, calendar jump, live scale widget (“≈2.5 Earths wide”, altitude), a live Earth↔Mars distance measure, and floating labels for Neptune, Pluto, JWST, ISS and Voyager 2.

Fell short

  • Invented geography. The continents are a convincing procedural approximation, not real Earth coastlines — beautiful, but it isn't the actual map.
  • No night side. A clean day-side arrival means the city-lights layer the brief lists never shows in this shot.
  • Moon out of frame. Luna is modelled and listed, but the Earth arrival doesn't compose it into the view.

GPT-5.6 Lunar

sol-atlas · “Deep Space Atlas”
Correct, but schematic

Got right

  • A legible atlas HUD. 19-object catalogue with filters, a focus panel with correct data (radius 6,371 km, gravity 9.81, mean temp 14 °C, orbit 365.256 days), NASA link, layer toggles, telemetry header.
  • Breadth of bodies. Eight planets plus Pluto, Halley's Comet, the Galilean moons, Titan, Phobos/Deimos, an asteroid belt and a drawn orbital plane.
  • Navigation works. The Quick-Navigation “Earth” control correctly selected and focused the planet.

Fell short

  • Earth has no surface. The 3D planet is a plain flat-shaded sphere — no oceans, clouds, continents or atmosphere. The pretty blue globe is only a decorative panel icon, not the render.
  • The camera missed. “Fly to Earth” left the Sun blowing out half the frame as a giant flat disc while Earth sits small and half in shadow — the opposite of a cinematic arrival.
  • Diagram, not AAA. Blocky square asteroid sprites and flat shading read as a schematic star-atlas, well short of the “feels like a modern game” bar the brief sets.
The read

Both nailed the data. Only one nailed the intent.

This brief is really two tests: be scientifically accurate and be cinematically beautiful. Both models passed the first — near-identical, correct reference data and a full sci-fi HUD. They split hard on the second. Fable 5 rendered a textured, cloud-wrapped, atmospherically-lit Earth and directed the camera to arrive on it like a shot in a game. GPT-5.6 Lunar built a competent atlas but left Earth an untextured sphere and let the Sun wreck the framing on the one navigation the user is most likely to try first.

The gap isn't knowledge — it's execution of the aesthetic and camera direction, exactly the part of “AAA” that can't be satisfied by getting the numbers right. It mirrors the coding-pillar story on the scoreboard: correct output is table stakes; the models separate on the demanding, hard-to-specify finish.
