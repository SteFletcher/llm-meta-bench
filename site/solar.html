Same prompt, three solar systems.

Nine builds were handed the identical brief — a sprawling spec for an “AAA-quality, browser-based, real-time Solar System simulator” that should feel like a game, not a website. These are the three highest-scoring submitted results: Claude Fable 5, Kimi K3 and Claude Sonnet 5. Each owned the whole build. To expose how their priorities differ, I used each app's own navigation to fly to Earth and captured the arrival.

Brief: solar-bench/PROMPT.md Stack asked: Three.js · TypeScript · Vite Target: cinematic · scientifically accurate Task: navigate → Earth → screenshot Field: top 3 of 9 assessed builds Axes: explicit execution · implicit intent

How this is scored. Explicit execution asks whether the build delivered the features, science, controls and verification the brief literally requested. Implicit intent asks whether those parts become the cinematic, credible, premium-game experience the brief was trying to create. The axes carry equal weight so checklist coverage cannot hide a lifeless world—and visual polish cannot hide a broken product. See the full scoring method →

Claude Fable 5

sol-simulator · “SOL”IN $10 · OUT $50 / MTok ↗

Got right

  • A believable ocean world. Procedural coast-like landmasses, blue specular oceans, layered drifting clouds and a thin atmospheric limb — rendered in-shader, not a flat sphere.
  • Cinematic framing. The fly-to arrived on the day side with Earth centered and fully lit — exactly the “leave it on a second monitor” feel the brief asked for.
  • Science is all there. Radius 6,371 km, gravity 9.81 m/s², escape velocity 11.19 km/s, orbital speed 29.78 km/s, axial tilt 23.44°, rotation 23.9 h, atmosphere N₂/O₂/Ar — correct and complete.
  • Deep HUD. Ten time scales with reverse, calendar jump, live scale widget (“≈2.5 Earths wide”, altitude), a live Earth↔Mars distance measure, and floating labels for Neptune, Pluto, JWST, ISS and Voyager 2.

Fell short

  • Invented geography. The continents are a convincing procedural approximation, not real Earth coastlines — beautiful, but it isn't the actual map.
  • No night side. A clean day-side arrival means the city-lights layer the brief lists never shows in this shot.
  • Moon out of frame. Luna is modelled and listed, but the Earth arrival doesn't compose it into the view.

Kimi K3

kimi3-solar-demo · “S·O·L”IN $3 · OUT $15 / MTok ↗

Got right

  • The most alive system. Eight thousand worker-built asteroids, two comets, solar wind, rotating clouds and six tours make this the strongest “leave it running” interpretation.
  • Serious engine breadth. Thirty focused modules, full catalogue, gamepad support, true-scale mode, live renderer counters and one of the deepest control surfaces in the field.
  • Strong arrival. Earth is deliberately day-lit, fills the frame and remains legible inside an unusually dense, game-like scene.
  • Science and checks hold. Build, lint and 16 unit tests pass; the physical data and Kepler propagation are sound.

Fell short

  • Invented surface data. Every planet uses a 512×256 generated canvas. Earth's continents and city-light density are synthetic, so deep zoom cannot pass the coastline test.
  • Visual excess. The cyan atmospheric shell, orbit graphics, giant asteroid belt and bright particles compete with the planet rather than stage it cleanly.
  • No browser suite. The README claims browser-automation verification, but no E2E suite ships with the build.

Claude Sonnet 5

sonnet5-solar-demo · “SOL”IN $2 · OUT $10 / MTok ↗

Got right

  • The only real Earth in the fair top three. Credited 2K day, night and cloud imagery produces actual coastlines, independent clouds and real population-density lights.
  • Smart asset judgment. It uses Solar System Scope and New Horizons maps instead of spending the visual budget inventing every world by hand.
  • Strong explicit coverage. The full catalogue, science model, time controls, camera modes, tours, HUD and accessibility work are all substantive.
  • Core checks pass. Production build, ESLint, test typecheck and 10 unit tests succeed.

Fell short

  • No reliable day-side staging. Earth's lit arrival was orbital luck; Jupiter and Saturn arrive as dark crescents.
  • Verification is unstable. The isolated production browser rerun passes only 7 of 29 declared cases under software rendering.
  • Scaffolding and hard-body gaps remain. Empty worker/asset directories, absent ring shadow and a barely visible Cassini division keep it below the two leaders.
The read

Fable wins the product. Sonnet wins the planet. Kimi keeps the system alive.

These are genuinely the field's top three, but they win different versions of the brief. Fable 5 is the most complete and verified product: the best joining of engine, interaction, science, camera direction and test coverage. Kimi K3 builds the broadest living game system, with the most motion and machinery around the planets. Sonnet 5 makes the smartest visual shortcut in the provenance-clean field: borrow the real universe, credit it, and let Earth look like Earth.

The ranking turns on where each build accepts compromise. Fable and Kimi invent geography; Sonnet borrows real surfaces but leaves camera direction and browser reliability unfinished. On the full rubric Fable's compromise is least damaging, so it leads at 9.1. Sonnet's real Earth earns third at 8.0.
