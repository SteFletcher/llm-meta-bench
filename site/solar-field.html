The intent-loss gallery.

Five builds were handed the same 569-line brief for a cinematic, “AAA-quality” Solar System simulator. Here is Earth from each — the most familiar test of whether factual completeness becomes a convincing experience. The science converges: all five know the radius, gravity and axial tilt, and all five shipped a full glass HUD. What doesn't converge is the planet itself. This is an editorial ordering by how much of the brief's actual intent — “make it beautiful” — survived contact with the build.

The pattern

The checklist got built. The vibe got lost.

The brief has two kinds of requirement. One kind is enumerable — a list of planets, a list of widgets, a table of physical constants, ten named time scales. The other is a single qualitative thesis, stated in the “Definition of Done”: it should feel like a premium game, beautiful enough to leave running on a second monitor. Every build satisfied the enumerable kind — that's why the HUDs and data panels are near-indistinguishable. They diverged entirely on the thesis.

That's intent loss. Long specs quietly reward the parts that can be checked off, and the un-checkable goal is the first thing to evaporate under the weight of shipping everything else. GPT-5.6 Sol is the sharpest illustration: it spent its polish budget on the most beautiful cockpit of the field and flew you to a planet that isn't recognizably Earth. Only Fable 5 fully kept the thesis — it made the world, not just the instruments around it, worth looking at.

It's the coding-pillar story again, in pixels: correct output is table stakes. Models separate on the demanding, hard-to-specify finish — and when the goal can't be graded, most of them lose it.
