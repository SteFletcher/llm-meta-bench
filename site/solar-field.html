The pattern

The checklist got built. The vibe got lost.

The brief has two kinds of requirement. One kind is enumerable — a list of planets, a list of widgets, a table of physical constants, ten named time scales. The other is a single qualitative thesis, stated in the “Definition of Done”: it should feel like a premium game, beautiful enough to leave running on a second monitor. Most builds did much better on the enumerable kind — HUDs, controls and physical constants — than on the thesis. Gemini did not even clear that baseline. The field still diverged most sharply on whether Earth felt like a destination.

That's intent loss. Long specs quietly reward the parts that can be checked off, and the un-checkable goal is the first thing to evaporate under the weight of shipping everything else. The provenance-clean leaders keep more of that thesis, but none is complete: Fable and Kimi invent geography, while Sonnet shows that borrowing and crediting real imagery can rescue the money shot even when camera direction and runtime verification remain unstable. Original GPT-5.6 Sol is the clearest intent-loss case: an exceptional cockpit wrapped around an abstract procedural Earth.

It's the coding-pillar story again, in pixels: correct output is table stakes. Models separate on the demanding, hard-to-specify finish — and when the goal can't be graded, most of them lose it.
